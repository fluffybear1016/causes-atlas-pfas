From the U.S. EPA · who is most at risk

"Pregnant and lactating women tend to drink more water per pound of body weight than the average person and as a result they may have higher PFAS exposure compared to other people if it is present in their drinking water."

Children

"Because children are still developing, they may be more sensitive to the harmful effects of chemicals such as PFAS. They can also be exposed more than adults because children drink more water, eat more food, and breathe more air per pound of body weight than adults, which can increase their exposure to PFAS."

Documented effects per EPA:
  • Reproductive effects such as decreased fertility or increased high blood pressure in pregnant women.
  • Developmental effects or delays in children, including low birth weight, accelerated puberty, bone variations, or behavioral changes.
  • Increased risk of some cancers, including prostate, kidney, and testicular cancers.
  • Reduced ability of the body's immune system to fight infections, including reduced vaccine response.
  • Interference with the body's natural hormones.
  • Increased cholesterol levels and/or risk of obesity.

The Hialeah question · why we can't yet prove the PFAS-fertility link at the ZIP-code level

Hialeah is 95% Hispanic, low-income, and sits directly downgradient of the MIA Airport AFFF plume. If PFAS were driving Miami-Dade reproductive outcomes, Hialeah's numbers should be the worst in the county.

Public ZIP-level birth-outcome data for Hialeah is not currently exposed by FLHealthCHARTS or PeriStats in a scrapable form. Florida CHARTS and Miami-Dade Healthy Start hold the data and require interactive queries (or public-records requests) to extract.

What we know from upstream signals: race/income are the dominant correlates of Miami-Dade reproductive outcomes in publicly accessible data — Liberty City and Overtown (Black, NOT downgradient of MIA) show worse maternal/infant health than Hialeah (Hispanic, downgradient of MIA). That doesn't disprove PFAS — it means PFAS is operating on top of a much larger race/income signal, and a confounding-adjusted analysis is needed to detect it.

The biomonitoring data the project actually needs is the FDOH PFAS panel cross-referenced with the FBDR hypospadias rate by ZIP and Healthy Start screen results by ZIP. That data does not yet exist publicly for Miami-Dade. The absence is itself a public-health data gap worth closing.

What the literature does NOT yet show — our honest list of unproven claims

The PFAS body-effects literature is large and growing, but several claims that circulate informally are not yet supported by peer-reviewed primary human studies, and Project Miami will not make them:

  • PFAS does not have a published human peer-reviewed link to hair loss or alopecia. Hair appears in the PFAS literature only as a measurement matrix (PFAS detected in hair), not as an outcome.
  • PFAS does not have a published human peer-reviewed link to acne. Chloracne is associated with dioxins and PCBs, which are chemically different from PFAS — do not conflate.
  • PFAS does not cause dental fluorosis. PFAS contains carbon-fluorine bonds but these are not bioavailable as free fluoride; dental fluorosis is a free-fluoride phenomenon. Chemically wrong.
  • No peer-reviewed PFAS → erectile dysfunction cohort study has been published. Lower testosterone is documented (above), so the mechanism is plausible — but the direct outcome has not been studied.
  • No primary human studies on PFAS & facial bone development, beard growth, voice changes, or muscle mass. These are inferable indirectly via testosterone suppression but not directly studied.

This is the literature as it stands in 2026. We expect each of these gaps will close over the next ten years. Until then, Project Miami stays inside what has been published.

The cluster pattern: what 13 conditions together tell us

If chronic PFAS exposure were producing harm in Miami-Dade, the bioaccumulation hypothesis predicts a cluster of simultaneous rises across immune, endocrine, metabolic, and developmental axes — not a single signal.

Of 13 tracked conditions: 10 are clearly rising, 2 are mixed, 1 is flat, 0 are declining. The four conditions with the strongest peer-reviewed PFAS mechanism in the population (pediatric and adult thyroid cancer, kidney cancer, testicular cancer, hypertensive disorders of pregnancy) all show rises.

This is the shape of a population under chronic environmental exposure. It is not proof of causation — Hispanic genetic susceptibility, the obesity epidemic, post-COVID inflection, and screening intensification are real confounders. But the pattern is consistent with what the PFAS literature predicts.

Where is the PFAS coming from? Every Florida airport · every military base · the same chemistry

PFAS in South Florida tap water originates upstream — in the groundwater that feeds the Biscayne Aquifer. The dominant source is aqueous film-forming foam (AFFF), used by every FAA Part 139 airport and every military airfield to suppress jet-fuel fires since the 1970s. Every facility that used AFFF for decades shows PFAS in groundwater at multiples of 1,000× to 1,000,000× the federal drinking-water MCL of 4 ppt.

What about Fort Lauderdale-Hollywood (FLL) and Palm Beach International (PBI)? Civilian airports are outside the DOD's PFAS reporting regime and Florida DEP's airport-specific investigations are still in early stages — so neither facility has a publicly released on-airport monitoring well number. But the downgradient evidence is unmistakable:

  • FLL → Dania Beach tap water: 124 ppt total ΣPFAS — the second-highest of 21 South Florida taps in the FIU/Quinete study (Li et al. 2022). Dania Beach pulls from the wellfield 1–2 miles downgradient of FLL, in the same Biscayne Aquifer system.
  • PBI is identified by multiple sources as the dominant local PFAS source for Palm Beach County drinking water, sitting inside the Palm Beach County Wellfield Protection Zone — but no on-airport ppt number has been published.
  • Hollywood, Hallandale Beach, Dania Beach, and Sunrise (all in Broward) received a combined ~$35 M in 2025 AFFF MDL settlements — i.e., the utilities themselves have legally asserted airport AFFF as the source of the PFAS in their finished water.
  • MIA-area tap water (2013): 47 ppt PFOS measured in Miami-Dade wells closest to MIA. Miami Beach (2014): 58 ppt PFOS.

The pattern is consistent across the state: every major airport and every military airfield used AFFF for fifty years; every one of them is now a documented or presumed plume source for the shallow aquifer underneath; every downgradient utility — including all four serving Miami-Dade — exceeds the April 2024 federal MCL today.

How does this translate to PFAS in your blood? (Model-predicted)

The peer-reviewed exposure-to-serum coefficient (Andrews & Naidenko, 2020, derived from C8 Science Panel pharmacokinetics) is approximately 0.14 ng/mL of serum PFOA per 1 ppt of PFOA in drinking water, at steady state with 5+ years of exposure. The number that follows is a model prediction, not a measurement.

For a Hialeah mother (16 ppt PFOA, 31 ppt PFOS) living there 5+ years:

  • Model-predicted serum PFOA uplift: ~2.2 ng/mL above the national female childbearing-age median of ~1.2–1.4 ng/mL (NHANES 2017–2020) — predicted total roughly 2.5–3× a typical US woman of childbearing age.
  • Model-predicted serum PFOS uplift: ~4–6 ng/mL above the national baseline of ~3 ng/mL — predicted total roughly 2–3× a typical US woman.

NASEM's 2022 clinical guidance recommends additional clinical follow-up when the sum of seven PFAS in serum reaches 2 ng/mL, and enhanced follow-up at 20 ng/mL. A Hialeah mother's model-predicted sum across these two congeners alone is ≈ 8–11 ng/mL — already inside the bracket NASEM names. The point of this calculation is decision-relevance: it is a number worth asking your physician to actually measure, not assume.

Source: NASEM 2022 · Andrews & Naidenko 2020 (EST Letters) · CDC NHANES 2017–2020.

Northwest Wellfield triple jeopardy — three confirmed AFFF / PFAS sites inside the same drinking-water capture zone

Miami-Dade WASD's Northwest Wellfield supplies roughly 150 million gallons per day to the Main System — the water that reaches Hialeah, Doral, Coral Gables, Pinecrest, Brickell, Key Biscayne, and (via wholesale) Miami Beach. Three confirmed PFAS source sites sit inside the wellfield's documented zone of contribution:

  1. Miami-Dade Fire Rescue HQ Doral — 9300 NW 41st St. — confirmed soil + groundwater PFAS over GCTL
  2. Miami-Dade College Fire Academy — 3180 NW 119th St., Opa-locka — 800 to 26,100 ppt across 10 monitor wells
  3. Miami-Dade Resources Recovery Facility (waste-to-energy) — 6990 NW 97th Ave., Doral — co-located with MDFR HQ; PFAS in MSW leachate is universal in EPA biosolids sampling nationwide but no Miami-Dade specific data yet published

All three sit upgradient of the wellfield draws. The County's federal complaint (Case 2:20-cv-04194-RMG) names AFFF and Miami-Dade Fire Rescue's own facilities as among the contamination sources WASD is now spending up to $3 billion to remediate.

The County tested. The County found contamination. The County took wells offline. The County waited five months. The County's official communication, when it came, emphasized "blending" — that the dirtier water was diluted with cleaner water until it averaged below the federal advisory. That argument depends on a specific assumption: that a Miami mother's pregnancy and her child's first 1,000 days of development care about the blended average, rather than what entered her tap on the day the dirty wells were running.

Families plan their lives on the public reassurance, not the federal pleadings. The gap between the two is, today, ninety-five wells wide and three billion dollars deep.

Why does Miami Beach's AWQR show higher PFAS than WASD's, for the same source water?

Both reports describe the same source water — the Biscayne Aquifer, drawn through WASD's Hialeah, John E. Preston, and Alexander Orr treatment plants — and both rely on the same underlying sampling program (federal UCMR5 monitoring, conducted by WASD using EPA Methods 533 and 537.1). They publish different numbers because each utility chose to present the data differently.

WASD's "Main System" column reports the highest single PFAS detection from the consolidated pool of Main System entry points and is effectively a blended-system maximum (31 ppt PFOS, 16 ppt PFOA). Miami Beach, which buys 100% of its water from WASD and is fed primarily by the older Hialeah and John E. Preston plants — the plants closest to historical AFFF firefighting-foam contamination from the Miami River industrial corridor and MIA — reports the highest detection at the entry point that specifically feeds the island (38 ppt PFOS, 30 ppt PFOA). WASD's own report internally corroborates this band — the South Dade subsystem shows 35 ppt PFOS and the REDAVO subsystem shows 35 ppt PFOS, so values in the 30s are present across the WASD system, not just on Miami Beach.

Neither number is an annual average; both are the highest single detection during the reporting period at the entry point to distribution. UCMR5 samples groundwater systems only twice per year, at the entry point, not at customer taps — so neither figure reflects what a child actually drinks at home.

The decision-relevant conclusion: a Miami Beach household should use the Miami Beach number (38 PFOS, 30 PFOA) as the planning value, because it describes the entry point that actually feeds the island. Both numbers point to the same action — even WASD's lower 31 ppt PFOS is 7.75× the EPA's 4 ppt federal MCL.

The legal record · what the cases actually established

Both settlements arise from the same federal court: In re Aqueous Film-Forming Foams Products Liability Litigation, MDL No. 2873, U.S. District Court for the District of South Carolina, Judge Richard M. Gergel. The class-action subset is case 2:23-cv-03230-RMG.

The defendants who paid: 3M (under its $10.3–$12.5B global class settlement, final-approved Feb. 2, 2024); DuPont, Chemours, and Corteva (under their $1.185B class settlement, final-approved Feb. 2024); Tyco Fire Products (~$750M, approved 2024); BASF Corporation (~$316.5M, approved 2024). Miami-Dade and the Broward cities each opted into all four class settlements as class members.

The legal theory established in federal court: Plaintiffs pled — and the court approved settlement on — five causes of action. (1) Strict product liability for design defect: PFAS-containing AFFF was unreasonably dangerous and safer alternatives existed. (2) Strict product liability for failure to warn: the manufacturers knew of PFAS toxicity decades before disclosure. (3) Negligence in design, manufacture, and sale. (4) Continuing trespass: PFAS migrating through groundwater into utility wells is an ongoing trespass on water-system property. (5) Public nuisance: the contamination interferes with the public right to safe drinking water. Miami-Dade additionally pled actual and constructive fraudulent transfer against DuPont, regarding the Chemours spinoff structured to insulate DuPont from PFAS liability.

At the time of Miami-Dade's complaint filing (Dec. 3, 2020): 70 of WASD's 95 wells had detectable PFAS. Six wells exceeded the (then-current) 70 ppt EPA health advisory. Three Miami Springs wells were taken offline. Three Hialeah wells remained online specifically to avoid spreading contamination through the aquifer.

The source named in the operative complaints: aqueous film-forming foam (AFFF) used at MIA, Opa-locka, Homestead Air Reserve Base, fire-training facilities, and other AFFF sites. Class counsel's own consent letter to the County: "contamination of Drinking Water supplies with PFAS primarily through AFFF (which is estimated to have caused the majority of the PFAS contamination in Drinking Water)."

The fight is no longer whether South Florida water has been contaminated by AFFF. The fight is no longer whether the polluters owe the money. Both questions are answered. The remaining fights are whether ~$234 million combined to Miami-Dade County and the four Broward cities is enough to fix what the carbon-fluorine bond has put into the Biscayne Aquifer — and whether the cumulative load already in residents' bodies will produce the cancers, the pregnancies, and the developmental harms the literature predicts.
